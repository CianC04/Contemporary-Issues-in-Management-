Chapter 08 - Management Theory

Five frameworks that explain the collapse

Manchester United's dysfunction is not just a football story - it is a textbook case study in modern management failure. Five theoretical lenses, one consistent finding: this is a structurally broken organisation.

Agency Theory - Jensen & Meckling, 1976
The owners are not acting in the club's interests
Agency Theory holds that a conflict exists between the principal (owner) and agent (operator) because each pursues their own self-interest. The Glazers extracted approximately ¬£1.2bn via debt interest, dividends and fees. Classic principal-agent misalignment: reward flows upward regardless of performance.
Verdict: Textbook principal-agent failure. The incentive structure actively punished on-pitch investment.
Stakeholder Theory - Freeman, 1984
Fans, players and staff treated as secondary
Freeman argues sustainable organisations must balance all stakeholders. United's ownership systematically inverted this. Shareholders extracted ¬£1.2bn. Fans paid rising prices watching deteriorating football. Staff were made redundant while Ratcliffe lived in Monaco to avoid UK tax.
Verdict: Total stakeholder neglect. Financial extraction prioritised over every other stakeholder claim.
Organisational Culture - Schein, 1985
Ferguson built a winning culture - dismantled in under two years
Schein identifies culture at three levels: artefacts, espoused values and underlying assumptions. Ferguson's 26-year tenure built culture at all three simultaneously. Ten managers in 13 years means no new culture ever had time to form.
Verdict: Cultural capital accumulated over 26 years destroyed in under 24 months. Never rebuilt.
Short-Termism - Laverty, 1996
Every decision optimised for the next 18 months
Short-termism is the systematic prioritisation of short-run outcomes over long-run value creation. Managers hired reactively and sacked before their projects matured. INEOS promised Amorim three years and terminated at 14 months. There is no evidence of a plan with a horizon beyond 18 months at any point since 2013.
Verdict: Chronic short-termism at every level. No decision-maker has operated with a credible long-term plan.
Contingency Theory - Lawrence & Lorsch, 1967
The structure does not match the environment
Contingency Theory holds that the optimal structure depends on the environment. The modern Premier League demands fast, integrated decision-making. United's structure delivers the opposite: two owners with divergent interests, a DoF who doubted the manager before hiring him, and a CEO caught between them.
Verdict: Structure-environment mismatch. The governance model is incompatible with competing at the top of the modern Premier League.

What the frameworks agree on

Five different theoretical lenses, one consistent finding: Manchester United's problems are structural and systemic, not tactical or personnel-based. Changing the manager does not fix an agency problem. Hiring a better DoF does not resolve a principal-agent conflict between two owners.

The solution requires unified ownership with aligned incentives, a governance structure matched to the competitive environment, a long-term planning horizon, and genuine stakeholder accountability. Under the current dual-ownership model, none of these conditions are met.

Carrick's results since January 2026 are genuine and encouraging. But they are happening inside a structure that every major management framework identifies as fundamentally broken. The results paper over the cracks. They do not fill them.

Chapter 09 - Analysis

INEOS are repeating the same mistakes

‚ö† Analysis and opinion - not verified fact

The most damning thing about United in early 2026 is not that the Glazers failed - it is that INEOS, brought in to fix the problem, are making structurally identical errors. Ratcliffe publicly promised Amorim "three years." He was sacked after 14 months. The Athletic reported that Director of Football Jason Wilcox had doubts about Amorim's system before INEOS chose to hire him.

The "hybrid model" - Ratcliffe controlling football operations while the Glazers retain 48.9% - has not produced clarity. It has produced a power vacuum. Dan Ashworth was appointed sporting director, spent five months on gardening leave from Newcastle, arrived at Old Trafford, and was dismissed within weeks. Nobody at United can explain with confidence who makes the final call on anything.

Michael Carrick's early results offer genuine hope. But they are happening in spite of the ownership structure, not because of it. The pattern - overpromise, under-deliver, blame the manager, reset - has not changed under INEOS. Only the names have.
